About

The purpose of this visualization is to visualise global CO2 emissions, their sources and it's effects on global temperature.

Firstly, this visualization will be exploring the trend of global CO2
emissions over time. We will then explore the sources of CO2 emissions. Then, the effects of CO2 emission on global temperature will be visualised.

Lastly, we will be exploring Malaysia's CO2 emissions and how it has affected the country's temperature

What is CO2 Emission?

CO2 emission is defined as the release of Carbon Dioxide
into the atmosphere

CO2 emission causes a variety of global problems. Most prominently,
global warming, which causes the greenhouse effect, resulting in
increasing global temperature.

From there, rising global temperatures causes a cascading array of problems

Keywords

Kilotons : A unit of weight, 1000 kilograms

Celsius : A unit of temperature

CO2 : Carbon dioxide

Emission : Release of a substance

Per capita : Average per person

CO2 Emission Source by Country
We can see that over the span of 59 years, Oil and Coal make up over 90% of the global CO2 Emissions. Majority of Malaysia's emissions are from Coal
3.
Sources of CO2 Emission by Continent in 2019
Europe has the highest CO2 emissions in 2019 followed closely by North America. Oil is the biggest contributor of CO2 emissions followed by Coal and Cement


4.
Yearly Average Global Temperature Fluctuation
There has been a significant increase in global temperature fluctuation since the turn of the century. The fluctuation average is the average temperature change per month. Belarus in particular, reported an average fluctuation of 2.98 degree Celsius in temperature in 2019.
5.
Correlation between Temperature and CO2 Emissions
In the line chart below, we can see that the CO2 emission per capita stays stagnant.
However, the global average fluctuation temperature still increases. This may be due to the CO2 emissions causing the greenhouse effect
6.
Correlation between GDP and CO2 Emissions
We can see that there is a positive correlation between CO2 Emissions and GDP per capita.
For example, China's GDP and CO2 Emissions both increase as time passes
